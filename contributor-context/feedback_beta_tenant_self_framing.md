**HARD CROSS-PROJECT RULE.** Locked 2026-05-22 PM during Collie's CreatorWheelhouse brief draft.

## The rule

When onboarding any Heye Lab beta tenant — Collie's CreatorWheelhouse, Brons, Joy, future tenants — keep two mental frames in parallel, and never let them cross:

- **Tenant's frame:** *"I'm building my perfect platform."* Sharp, first-person, opinionated, self-interested. Every "I want X" / "X annoys me" / "this should work like Y" is high-signal input.
- **Our private frame:** *"We're building a generalizable product, using the tenant's real usage as canonical beta."* Quiet. Behind the curtain. Never surfaced in tenant-facing copy, briefs, or conversations.

## Why crossing the frames degrades signal

If a tenant starts thinking *"what would other users want?"* their input pre-filters through an imagined customer base they cannot actually predict. Their decisions stop being sharp. We lose the canonical-honest signal that lets the framework abstractions emerge correctly.

PAL is the proof of the pattern in reverse: PAL operators built for Port A's specific reality, not for "small coastal towns." HeyeDeploy patterns emerged *after the fact* from the shapes that worked in PAL's first-person build. The canonical-becomes-product flow only works if the canonical was built honestly for itself.

## Operational rules

1. **Briefs to beta tenants** describe the build in terms of their use case + benefits to them. Never "...and this will be a product we sell to others."
2. **Build decisions** weight the tenant's first-person input, not "would other users like this." Generalization is our problem to solve later, at the framework layer — not during the tenant build.
3. **No "what about other Etsy sellers / other beach towns / other agencies?" questions to the tenant.** Their job is to be themselves loudly.
4. **Not deception.** Beta tenants know HeyeLab is a business and that they're collaborators. The rule is about keeping their input self-interested, not hiding the business.
5. **When product framing becomes load-bearing internally** (e.g. when we start scoping CreatorDeploy as a public-facing product), keep that work in our private docs / sessions — don't ask the tenant to weigh in on positioning until the public product is ready to attribute them as co-creator.

## Reference instances

- **PAL canonical → HeyeDeploy:** PAL operators built for Port A. Framework abstractions surfaced later from what worked. The reverse path (build for hypothetical many → squeeze into one tenant) would have failed.
- **Brons / Joy / future tenants:** same posture — they're building their business, framework is our backend concern.
- **Collie's CreatorWheelhouse:** first explicit application of the rule. Brief was rewritten without the product-framing line before send.

## Revisit conditions

- If a beta tenant *spontaneously* asks "could other people use this?" — they've opened the door, address truthfully, but don't recenter their build decisions around hypothetical others.
- Once a product is publicly named + positioned (e.g. CreatorDeploy launches), the line softens — beta tenants become attributed co-creators. The rule applies primarily to the *pre-public beta window*.
